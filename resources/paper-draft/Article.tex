\begin{abstract}
	Sjögren's Syndrome (SS) and Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE) are chronic systemic autoimmune diseases; aberrant immune activation, autoantibody production, and end-organ inflammation are common characteristics. SS affects approximately 0.1–0.4\% of the general population; SLE affects 20–150 per 100,000 individuals globally, both with a striking 9:1 female predominance and significant systemic disease burden. Current therapeutic options remain inadequate; no FDA-approved disease-modifying therapies exist for SS, and while advances have been made in SLE management, a substantial proportion of patients continue to experience disease flares and progressive organ damage. Critically, no approved therapy directly targets the complement cascade in either disease, representing a significant therapeutic gap.
	Dysregulation of the complement cascade has long been implicated as a central pathogenic driver in both SS and SLE, contributing to immune complex accumulation, type I interferon signaling, and progressive tissue injury. Aberrant activation of terminal complement proteins drives the pathogenesis of SS and SLE through dysregulation of the alternative pathway, a constitutively active cascade that serves as a critical amplification loop for complement activation. Perturbations of the alternative pathway enable the overproduction of proinflammatory mediators by impairing regulatory control over the central complement proteins C3 and C5. Furthermore, differential expression of complement receptors CR1, CR2, CR3, and CR4 in SS and SLE perpetuates this pro-inflammatory milieu, amplifying immune-mediated tissue injury.

\section*{Introduction}
%\Blindtext
	Sjögren's Syndrome (SS) and Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE) are chronic systemic autoimmune diseases that collectively affect millions of individuals worldwide; both are observed disproportionately in women and minority populations. The global incidence rate of SS is approximately 7 per 100,000 person-years \cite{Reyes_Jaraba_2022}, with higher incidence and prevalence rates observed in women and older age groups \cite{Thurtle2024}; onset typically occurs in the fourth and fifth decade of life. Beyond the distinctive sicca symptoms, SS carries significant systemic burden; a 2021 meta-analysis demonstrated a nearly 50\% increase in mortality among patients with primary Sjögren's syndrome (pSS), with older age, male gender, vasculitis, hypocomplementaemia, positive anti-La/SSB, and cryoglobulinaemia identified as adverse prognostic factors \cite{10.1093/rheumatology/keab364}. The global incidence rate of SLE is approximately 5.14 per 100,000 person-years, with the rate differing markedly between sexes; for men the rate is 1.53 per 100,000 person-years while for women this rate increases to 8.82 per 100,000 person-years \cite{Tian_2023}. SLE predominantly affects young women, with Black, Hispanic, and Asian populations experiencing increased prevalence, incidence, and morbidity \cite{Barber_2021}. Lupus nephritis (LN) is the most common cause of kidney injury in those afflicted with SLE, occurring in approximately 50\% of patients; male patients tend to have more aggressive disease with higher rates of renal and cardiovascular involvement \cite{Parikh_2020}. There is an established clinical and serological overlap between SS and SLE; co-occurrence rates are approximately 23\%, with the SLE-SS group being older and more female \cite{ruacho:2020aa}; both diseases share similar anti-SSA and anti-SSB autoantibody profiles, hypergammaglobulinemia, and complement consumption \cite{Eriksson_2011}. Despite their prevalence and significant morbidity, current therapeutic options for both diseases remain inadequate. No effective disease-modifying therapy has been approved for SS, with current treatment largely focused on symptom management; clinical trials on targeted drugs have frequently failed due to an inability to demonstrate efficacy from misidentified targets or inconsistent research outcomes. For SLE, advancements have been made in disease management through therapeutics such as belimumab and anifrolumab; however, the complexity of clinical diagnosis and disease management remains a major challenge, with an over-reliance on corticosteroid therapy contributing to long-term organ damage and substantial morbidity. Critically, no approved therapy directly targets the complement cascade in either SS or SLE, representing a significant and unmet therapeutic need.

	The complement system is a critical arm of innate immunity comprising over 50 soluble and membrane-bound proteins that function in a tightly regulated cascade. Activation proceeds through three distinct pathways — the classical, lectin, and alternative pathways — all converging at the cleavage of the central complement protein C3 into C3a, a potent anaphylatoxin, and C3b, a major opsonin, which subsequently drives cleavage of C5 into C5a and C5b, initiating assembly of the membrane attack complex (MAC). The complement activation via the alternative pathway has been recognized as a major pathogenic mechanism in autoimmune conditions; the alternative pathway is constitutively active and serves as a critical amplification loop for complement activation regardless of the initiating pathway \cite{Legatowicz_Koprowska_2020}. Tight regulation of this amplification loop by factor H, factor I, and membrane-bound regulatory proteins is essential to prevent self-tissue damage; perturbation of these regulators permits unchecked C3 and C5 activation with consequent inflammatory tissue injury.
	
	Dysregulation of the complement cascade has long been implicated as a central pathogenic driver in both SS and SLE. A genetic pattern involving partial deficiencies of C2 and C4A in the classical complement pathway has been demonstrated to be a strong risk factor for both SLE and primary SS, emphasizing the central role of the complement system in the pathogenesis of both diseases. PubMed In SLE, the pathogenic role of complement is paradoxical — inherited deficiencies of classical pathway components impair the tolerogenic clearance of apoptotic debris, yet complement simultaneously drives inflammatory tissue injury when aberrantly activated by the immune complexes that accumulate as a consequence of this clearance failure. Patients with SLE have relative deficiencies of the C3b/C4b receptor CR1 (CD35) on erythrocytes; this receptor mediates the binding, transport, and endocytosis of circulating immune complexes, and its deficiency is fundamental to the pathogenesis of SLE. PubMed In SS, the characteristic anti-SSA/Ro52 autoantibodies suggest complement activation via the classical pathway through immune complex formation and tissue damage; moreover, a decrease in serum C3 and C4 levels is detected in over 20\% of SS patients, indicating the role of innate immunity in SS pathogenesis. PubMed Central
	
	Central to the complement dysregulation in both diseases is the differential expression of complement receptors CR1, CR2, CR3, and CR4. The rs1143679 variant of ITGAM, encoding the R77H variant of CD11b (part of complement receptor 3; CR3), is among the strongest genetic susceptibility effects in human SLE PubMed; this variant impairs CR3-mediated phagocytosis, adhesion, and regulatory suppression of innate immune signaling, providing human genetic validation of CR3 as a mechanistically relevant target. CR2 (CD21) lowers the activation threshold of autoreactive B cells by co-stimulating the B cell receptor in the presence of C3d-opsonized autoantigens, directly amplifying the autoantibody production characteristic of both SS and SLE. C5aR1 is a potent anaphylatoxin receptor that increases vascular permeability, induces oxidative bursting and release of proinflammatory cytokines in myeloid cells, and exerts chemotactic effects on myeloid and lymphoid cells ScienceDirect; its dysregulation drives NET formation and perpetuates the autoantigen burden in SLE. Collectively, the dysregulation of these receptors sustains a self-amplifying pro-inflammatory milieu that current therapies are insufficiently equipped to interrupt.

% Current state of therapies
	Despite the well-established pathogenic role of complement in SS and SLE, no approved therapy directly targets complement receptors in either disease. The C5a receptor inhibitor avacopan has demonstrated superior sustained remission at 52 weeks compared to standard prednisone taper in ANCA-associated vasculitis New England Journal of Medicine, providing proof-of-concept clinical validation for C5aR1 blockade in complement-mediated disease; however, this agent remains inadequately studied in SS and SLE. Notably, avacopan is currently under regulatory scrutiny due to data integrity concerns in the pivotal ADVOCATE trial The Rheumatologist, underscoring the need for robust mechanistic evidence to guide the development of complement receptor-directed therapies. The transcriptional landscape of complement receptor expression in SS and SLE has not been comprehensively characterized, limiting the ability to identify the most actionable therapeutic targets. Using publicly available RNA-seq datasets sourced from NCBI Gene Expression Omnibus (GEO), this study performs differential gene expression and pathway enrichment analyses comparing SS and SLE patients to healthy controls, with the aim of characterizing complement receptor dysregulation at the transcriptional level and providing molecular rationale for targeted complement receptor blockade as a therapeutic strategy in SS and SLE.

\section*{Discussion}
	Our findings demonstrate that complement anaphylatoxin receptors are persistently upregulated in both central and peripheral target organs in chronic autoimmune disease such as sjogrens. This pattern underscores the dual nature of complement, vital for homeostasis but deleterious when uncontrolled.
	The strong expression in microglia parallels reports implicating complement in synaptic pruning and neurodegeneration, while renal upregulation aligns with complement driven glomerular and tubulointerstitial injury. Therapeutic inhibition of C3aR/C5aR signaling has shown benefit in preclinical models, and our transcriptomic results lend human relevance to these approaches.
	Emerging C3aR antagonists and C5aR inhibitors (e.g., avacopan) may thus hold promise for neuro-renal protection in Sjögren’s. Integrating receptor profiling with spatial transcriptomics and proteomic validation will refine patient stratification for complement-targeted therapy.

\section*{Conclusions}
	Complement receptors C3aR and C5aR are upregulated in autoimmune brain and kidney, bridging innate immunity and chronic inflammation. Their distribution across immune and parenchymal compartments identifies them as rational targets for tissue-directed complement modulation.
